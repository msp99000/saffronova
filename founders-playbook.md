## 1. What "India-only" changes (the good news)

Dropping the export/global ambition removes most of the hard problems from the original plan:

- **No Amazon-US block, no sanctions-export exposure.** You sell on Amazon.in, Flipkart, your own site, and to local/B2B buyers — all domestic, all fine.
- **You may not need to import at all.** For an India-only business, the smartest beginner move is usually to **buy already-imported saffron from domestic wholesalers** (e.g., Delhi's Khari Baoli spice market and established importers) rather than importing yourself. That means **no IEC, no customs duty, no customs broker, and crucially no Iran-payment headache** — you just buy in rupees from an Indian seller. You move to direct import later, only once volume justifies it.
- **Light output tax.** Saffron is **5% GST** (HSN 0910 / 09102010). It's collected on top of your price and largely passes through (you claim input credit), so it doesn't crush margins.

**The one decision that still matters most: where you buy.**

| Sourcing route | Need IEC/customs? | Iran-payment problem? | FSSAI licence needed | Capital tied in inventory | Best for |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Domestic wholesale** (buy imported saffron in India) | No | No | **State Licence** | Lower | **Starting out** ✅ |
| **Direct import** (Iran via UAE intermediary / Afghan) | Yes | Iran: yes; Afghan: minimal | **Central Licence** | Higher | Later, at volume |

**My recommendation:** launch on **domestic wholesale sourcing** (Afghan or Iranian saffron already landed in India, or Kashmiri if you want the premium GI story). Put your capital into **brand and marketing**, not into wrestling with customs and Iranian banks as first-time founders. Revisit direct import in year 2 when your volumes make the ~30% duty saving worth the operational pain.

## 2. ⚠️ The issue you must resolve before anything else: who can legally be an owner

This is the single most important thing in this document, and it's the kind of thing that sinks partnerships quietly. **Three of your four founders have day jobs that legally restrict business ownership.**

**The rule:** Government and quasi-government employees in India are barred from engaging — directly *or indirectly* — in any trade or business, including holding a **directorship or partnership**, without prior written sanction from their employer. Rule 15 of the Central Civil Services (Conduct) Rules, 1964 prohibits government servants from engaging in any trade, business, or employment without prior government permission; direct involvement (running a business, partnership, or directorship) is banned, and indirect involvement through family members must be declared and must not conflict with official duties. Even passive partnership is restricted — a government employee cannot own a business even as a sleeping or silent partner if it involves financial risk or control, and indirect investment in any trade or business is prohibited; what is allowed is passive income such as listed shares, mutual funds, rent, or interest.

How this lands on each of you:

- **Bhoopendra (NABARD).** NABARD has its **own published Employee Code of Conduct**, and it defines "commercial employment" to include a directorship of a company or partnership in a firm engaged in trade or business. Treat Bhoopendra as **restricted**: he most likely **cannot be a director or partner** without NABARD's written permission, and even a silent stake is doubtful. He must read NABARD's conduct rules and, if he wants in, formally seek sanction.
- **Mohit (PhD Professor).** If he teaches at a **government/public university**, the same state conduct rules apply — restricted. Professors can often do *consulting/research* with permission, but running a spice company is "business." If he's at a **private university**, his employment contract governs (many also restrict outside business or require disclosure). He must check his specific terms.
- **Rohit (bank, Canada).** Canadian banks have strict **outside-business-activity and conflict-of-interest policies**; he'd need to disclose and get approval, and a director role in an import/trading firm may be refused. Separately, as an **NRI**, his investment into an Indian company is allowed under FEMA but has **reporting requirements** — manageable, but do it properly.
- **Manpreet.** If he has **no restrictive employment** (or is willing to go full-time on this), he is likely the **one founder who can be the active operating director without an issue**.

### What this means structurally

You should not assume a tidy "four equal directors" company. The realistic, clean structures are:

1. **Operating founders + investors.** **Manpreet (and Mohit, if cleared) are the directors/operators.** **Rohit and Bhoopendra come in as investors and advisors, not directors.** This keeps everyone on the right side of their conduct rules.
2. **Decouple capital from equity (the clever move for Bhoopendra).** A restricted founder who wants exposure can contribute money as a **loan to the company at a fair interest rate** rather than as equity/partnership. **Interest income is generally treated as permissible passive income for a government employee**, whereas a directorship/partnership is not. It's cleaner than equity — but still get it cleared with NABARD, because lending to a business you're connected to can attract scrutiny.
3. **Permission route.** Anyone who genuinely wants equity and an active role applies to their employer for written sanction *before* incorporation. Don't do it retroactively.

> **Do not** use the "hold it in my wife's name" workaround casually. It's a recognised grey area and is only acceptable if the employee puts in **no money**, does **no management**, and uses **no official influence** — which usually defeats the purpose, and misreporting it is itself a conduct violation.

**Action:** Each of the three should get a one-line written answer from their employer/HR (or a labour-law advocate) on "Can I (a) be a director, (b) be a shareholder/partner, (c) lend money to a private company?" *before* you incorporate. This 2-week step protects all of you.

## 3. Choosing the entity

| Feature | Private Limited | LLP | Partnership Firm | Proprietorship |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Best when | You'll raise money / add investors | Simple, few partners, low compliance | Very simple, trust-based | Solo |
| Liability | Limited | Limited | **Unlimited** | **Unlimited** |
| Outside investment | Easiest (shares) | Harder | Hard | No |
| Compliance cost/yr | Higher (₹15–40k) | Lower (₹8–15k) | Lowest | Lowest |
| Credibility (banks, Amazon, suppliers) | Highest | High | Medium | Low |
| Adding/removing partners | Clean (share transfer) | Moderate | Messy | N/A |

**Recommendation: Private Limited company.** It gives limited liability (important — saffron inventory is valuable and adulteration claims are a real risk), it's the cleanest vehicle for the "operators + investors" structure in §2, it lets restricted founders hold equity *if* they get permission or come in via loans, and it's the only structure investors and lenders take seriously if you scale. The slightly higher compliance cost is worth it.

## 4. Stakeholding / equity — options, with worked examples

This is where friendships die. Spend real time here and **put it in writing**. First, the principles a seasoned founder lives by:

**Five things that earn equity** (capital is only one of them):
1. **Full-time operating work over years** — by far the most valuable, because it's sustained risk and effort, not a one-time cheque.
2. **Capital** — money in.
3. **Skills / IP** — Manpreet's tech build, Mohit's R&D and lab credibility.
4. **Risk** — personal guarantees on loans, taking a pay cut.
5. **Network / origination** — Rohit had the idea; Bhoopendra's institutional access.

**The cardinal rule:** *Sweat outranks capital.* A founder grinding full-time for three years should end up owning meaningfully more than someone who wrote an equal cheque and watched. Equal capital does **not** mean equal equity.

### Three models

**Model A — Equal split (25 / 25 / 25 / 25).**
Simple and friendly, but dangerous unless capital *and* effort are genuinely equal. Two failure modes: (i) the full-time operator silently resents carrying three passive partners on equal equity; (ii) **4-way deadlock** — nobody can decide anything. If you ever use this, you *must* bolt on a clear CEO, defined decision rights, and salaries for whoever actually works (see below).

**Model B — Equal capital, role-weighted equity.**
Everyone contributes equal money, but equity reflects active contribution. This is usually the right answer for a friends' venture.

**Model C — Operators + Investors (fits your conduct-rules reality).**
Operating founders hold the controlling equity and run it; restricted/passive founders hold smaller equity (if permitted) or come in via loans, plus an advisory role. This is the structure I'd steer you toward given §2.

### A worked illustration (Model B/C blend)

*Assumptions (replace with your real ones): total ₹15 L raised, contributed equally (₹3.75 L each). Manpreet is full-time operator + builds the tech. Mohit is part-time active on R&D/quality (assume cleared). Rohit is the idea originator + part-time finance/advisory (passive due to job). Bhoopendra contributes via **loan**, not equity, and advises (due to NABARD rules).*

| Founder | Role | Capital | How they participate | Illustrative equity |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Manpreet** | Full-time operator, CEO, tech | ₹3.75 L equity | Director + shareholder | **40%** |
| **Mohit** | Part-time active, R&D/quality | ₹3.75 L equity | Director + shareholder (if cleared) | **25%** |
| **Rohit** | Originator, finance/advisory | ₹3.75 L equity | Shareholder + advisor (non-director) | **25%** |
| **Bhoopendra** | Advisor; capital via loan | ₹3.75 L **as loan** @ ~10% | Lender + advisor; **10% equity only if NABARD permits**, else 0% and his ₹3.75 L stays a loan repaid with interest | **10% or 0%** |

If Bhoopendra cannot hold equity, redistribute his 10% (e.g., Manpreet 45%, Mohit 27%, Rohit 28%) **or** keep it in an unissued pool, and his money is simply a loan the company repays first. The exact percentages are *yours to negotiate* — the point is the **method**: weight by sustained role first, capital second.

### Non-negotiable mechanics (put these in the agreement)

- **Vesting — even among friends.** Equity vests over **4 years with a 1-year cliff**. If someone walks after 8 months, they don't keep a quarter of the company. This single clause prevents the most common founder blow-up.
- **Roles & a single decision-maker.** Name **one CEO** (likely Manpreet) with authority over day-to-day decisions up to a threshold. Reserve big decisions (raising money, taking loans, selling, issuing equity, annual budget) for a board/shareholder vote. **You cannot run a company by 4-way consensus** — it will paralyse you.
- **Salary vs. dividend.** **Operators draw a salary** for the work (deferred/minimal at first to protect runway). **Equity returns come as dividends/exit**, shared by ownership %. This is the fair way to compensate the person doing the work without distorting ownership.
- **Capital as loan vs. equity.** Decide which founder money is **equity** (permanent, at risk, returns via dividends) vs. a **loan** (repaid first, with interest). Document it. (This is also the mechanism that lets Bhoopendra participate cleanly.)
- **Exit / leaver clauses.** What happens if someone wants out, stops contributing, or dies? Pre-agree a **buyback formula** (e.g., fair-value or a set multiple of contribution) so you're not negotiating during a crisis.
- **IP assignment.** Anything Manpreet builds (website, code, brand assets) and Mohit's R&D is **assigned to the company**, not owned personally.
- **Reserved matters & deadlock resolution.** List decisions needing supermajority, and a tie-breaker mechanism.

## 5. Budgeting strategy (the philosophy before the numbers)

Hard-won principles, then the spreadsheet:

1. **Cash is oxygen; inventory is where founders drown.** Saffron is ~₹250–600 *per gram* — it is dangerously easy to sink your whole budget into stock that then sits unsold while you have no money to *market* it. **Cap inventory at ~30–35% of your starting capital.** Buy small, sell, reorder. Slow-moving saffron also ages (2–3 yr shelf life).
2. **Protect ~6 months of runway.** Most of your first-year spend is *marketing and patience*, not product. Budget for the gap before break-even, not just the launch.
3. **Spend on the three things customers actually judge:** authenticity proof (lab report on pack), packaging that looks premium, and trust signals (reviews, content). Underspend on everything else.
4. **Keep founder salaries minimal at first.** The operator takes a small stipend; real reward comes later via dividends/value. This extends runway dramatically.
5. **Separate one-time setup from monthly burn** in your head and your sheet. Confusing them is how people miscalculate break-even.
6. **Hold a 10–15% contingency.** Something always costs more (a rejected packaging batch, a failed ad month).

## 9. Break-even & realistic timeline

- **Operating break-even:** ~₹2.5–3.5 L revenue/month (selling ~550–750 g).
- **Time to reach it consistently:** **9–18 months** — saffron is a trust purchase; reviews and repeat customers build slowly.
- **Payback on the ~₹14 L:** **18–36 months** in a realistic case. Treat faster as upside.
- **Plan ≥18 months of runway/patience.** This is a compounding brand, not a quick flip.

5. **Angels** — only after real traction (strong repeat rate, growing revenue). Not on the idea.
6. **Avoid** raising equity from anyone before the founders' agreement and conduct-rules questions are settled.

## 12. The 50-year-veteran's cheat-sheet (things people learn the hard way)

- **Settle equity, roles, and the leaver clause *before* you spend a rupee.** The friendship is the real asset; protect it with paperwork.
- **Never sink your budget into inventory.** Buy small, sell, reorder. Dead stock is dead cash.
- **One hero product, done beautifully, beats twelve mediocre SKUs.** Add range only after the first sells.
- **You compete on trust, not price.** A printed lab report, a clean origin story (honest — never fake "Kashmiri"), and real reviews are worth more than a discount.
- **Own your customer.** Push buyers to your website and email list; marketplaces rent you reach but keep the relationship and take ~18%.
- **Cash flow ≠ profit.** You can be "profitable" on paper and still die because money is stuck in stock and receivables. Watch the bank balance weekly.
- **Reputation is one bad batch away from zero.** In saffron, adulteration or a quality scandal is fatal. Test everything; document everything.
- **Don't quit your jobs yet.** Keep the salaries; run this lean until the numbers prove themselves. (This also happens to align with the conduct-rules reality.)
- **Decide who's the boss.** Equal partners with no leader is a recipe for slow death by indecision.
- **Get the boring stuff right:** GST filings on time, FSSAI valid, trademark filed early, contracts in writing. Boring compliance is cheaper than fixing a mess later.

## 13. Your first 60 days (sequenced)

1. **Days 1–14:** Each restricted founder gets written clarity from their employer on directorship/shareholding/loans (§2). Agree the equity model and leaver/vesting terms in principle.
2. **Days 10–20:** Engage a CS+CA. Incorporate the Pvt Ltd with the cleared structure. Sign the founders' agreement. Apply for GST + Udyam.
3. **Days 15–30:** Shortlist brand names → run through IP India Public Search (Class 30) → file the trademark. Apply for FSSAI State Licence.
4. **Days 20–35:** Vet 2–3 domestic wholesale suppliers; buy samples; lab-test them; pick your grade and supplier.
5. **Days 25–45:** Finalise brand, one hero SKU, packaging design; set up the website.
6. **Days 35–50:** Place first ~1.5 kg order; repackage under FSSAI-compliant conditions; shoot product photos.
7. **Days 45–60:** Soft-launch on website + Amazon.in; seed first reviews; begin small ad tests; pitch a few local/B2B/gifting buyers for early cash flow.
8. **Then:** review SKU-level margin, CAC, repeat rate monthly. Double down on what works before adding anything new.
