Saffron Trading Start-up — End-to-End Playbook

Prepared for: Manpreet, Rohit, Mohit & Bhoopendra Date: June 2026

Important caveats. This is a research-based planning document, not legal, tax, or investment advice. Numbers are illustrative ranges built from publicly available 2025–2026 data; real figures depend on your sourcing, pricing, and execution, and saffron prices are highly volatile. Two things you should pay for professionally before committing capital: (1) a one-time consult with a trade-compliance / sanctions lawyer (because of the Iran angle), and (2) a CA/company secretary for registrations and GST. Currency assumption: ~₹85 = US$1.

1. The honest assessment of the plan

What's right about it

  • Real, durable demand. Saffron is the world's most expensive spice and demand from food, pharma, cosmetics, and nutraceutical buyers keeps rising.
  • A genuine cost arbitrage. Iran produces ~85–90% of global supply and its wholesale price is a fraction of Kashmiri saffron.
  • D2C-friendly. Saffron is light, high-value, non-perishable for 2–3 years, and sells well online — ideal for a small team.
  • Your team is unusually well-suited (see §10).

What's wrong with the specific plan ("Iran → repackage → Amazon → global") — these are the three things to fix:

  1. Amazon prohibits Iranian-origin goods. Repackaging does not change country of origin. You can sell on Amazon.in (India), but not on Amazon US, and the global-Amazon ambition is largely blocked while the saffron is Iranian.
  2. Paying Iran is the hardest operational problem. Banking sanctions make direct payment to Iran impractical for a small importer. The realistic route is a UAE-based intermediary/re-exporter, with airtight certificate-of-origin documentation.
  3. Rohit's bank job is a compliance liability. A Canadian bank employee being an owner/director of an Iran-trading entity is a conflict-of-interest and sanctions-exposure problem. Keep Rohit on the India-side finance / fundraising, never on the Iran payment leg.

The strategic fork — decide this first:

Option Sourcing Sanctions / payment pain Can sell on Amazon US / export? Cost per gram Brand story
A. India-domestic only Iranian (via UAE intermediary) High No (India only) Lowest "Premium Persian"
B. Make-in-India Kashmiri (GI) or other Indian None Yes Highest "Pure Kashmiri / Indian" (strongest)
C. Afghan Afghan (India gives import concessions) Low Yes Medium "Heirloom Afghan"

Recommendation: If your real goal is global/export sales, start with B or C. Iranian (Option A) only makes sense if you deliberately stay India-domestic and solve the payment leg cleanly. You can also blend: launch domestically on Indian/Afghan saffron to build the brand and cash flow, and revisit Iranian sourcing later only if the economics demand it. Never label non-Kashmiri saffron as "Kashmiri" — that is GI infringement and fraud.

2. Legal structure — the foundation

Before you can import or sell food, you need a legal entity and a stack of registrations. Do these roughly in this order.

  1. Form a company. For 4 founders, a Private Limited company (cleaner for raising money and adding investors later) or an LLP (cheaper, simpler, lower compliance) are the two sensible choices. A Pvt Ltd is the better default if you ever want outside investment. Draft a founders' / shareholders' agreement on day one — equity splits, roles, vesting, what happens if someone exits. With four friends and uneven contributions, this is the single most important document for keeping the friendship intact.
    • Cost: ₹7,000–25,000 incl. professional fees (LLP at the lower end).
  2. PAN & TAN for the company (done as part of incorporation).
  3. GST registration. Mandatory for selling on marketplaces and for inter-state trade. Cost: government fee ₹0; CA fee ~₹2,000–5,000.
  4. Current account & AD Code. A business bank account, plus an Authorised Dealer (AD) Code from your bank registered at your port of import — required for customs clearance of imports.
  5. Udyam (MSME) registration — free, and it unlocks the 50% discount on trademark government fees and access to MSME schemes/loans. Do this early.

3. Import licences & food-safety certifications

This is the "what do we actually need" answer.

3.1 Import Export Code (IEC) — mandatory to import

  • Issued by the DGFT (Directorate General of Foreign Trade).
  • Without an IEC you cannot legally clear an import shipment.
  • Cost: government fee ₹500; with a consultant ~₹1,500–2,500. One-time, lifetime (with annual update).

3.2 FSSAI Central Licence — mandatory for you

  • FSSAI registration/licensing is compulsory for saffron exporters, re-packers and traders. Because you are importing and repackaging, you fall under the Central Licence category (importers require a Central Licence regardless of turnover).
  • Cost: government fee ₹7,500/year (you choose 1–5 years validity); with a consultant ~₹10,000–25,000 for setup.
  • You'll need: premises layout plan, equipment list, water-test report, and your other registrations.

3.3 Customs duty (HSN code 0910) — a major cost

  • Saffron sits under HSN 0910 (specifically 09102090).
  • Import duty into India is significant — reported around 30% historically, plus applicable surcharges, plus IGST (IGST is creditable against your output GST, so it's a cash-flow cost, not a permanent one).
  • Action: confirm the current effective duty for HSN 0910 20 90 with a customs broker (CHA) before you model margins — duty schedules change and this number drives your landed cost.
  • CHA fees: ~₹5,000–15,000 per shipment.

3.4 Other import documents

  • Certificate of Origin (critical — proves the supply chain, especially if routed via UAE)
  • Phytosanitary certificate (plant-origin product)
  • Commercial invoice & packing list
  • Bill of Lading / Airway Bill (saffron ships by air, not sea — sea isn't worth it for high-value, low-weight cargo)
  • Import insurance

3.5 Lab testing & quality standards

  • Saffron is the most adulterated spice in the world — this is both a risk and a marketing opportunity.
  • Test every batch against ISO 3632 (the international saffron standard, measuring crocin = colour, picrocrocin = taste, safranal = aroma) and FSSAI norms.
  • Cost: ~₹3,000–10,000 per batch at an accredited lab (e.g., NABL-accredited).
  • Putting a third-party lab report / crocin score on your packaging is one of the cheapest trust-builders available and differentiates you instantly.

3.6 If you ever export (Option B/C)

  • APEDA registration (Agricultural & Processed Food Products Export Development Authority) — RCMC certificate.
  • Phytosanitary certificate per shipment, plus destination-country food rules (e.g., FDA prior notice for the US, CFIA for Canada, EU food-import rules).
  • This is also where Bhoopendra's NABARD background and Mohit's lab credibility become real assets.

4. The bottlenecks (read this twice)

In rough order of how much they'll hurt:

  1. Paying Iran (Option A only). No clean banking channel for a small importer. Realistic path: a UAE/Dubai intermediary who re-exports, paid in a convertible currency, with full origin documentation. Adds 10–25% to cost and real due-diligence burden. Avoid informal/hawala channels entirely — that's where legal trouble lives.
  2. Sanctions exposure & Rohit's employer. Get the compliance lawyer consult. Structure ownership so Rohit is insulated from the Iran leg. If you go Option B/C, this bottleneck largely disappears.
  3. Working capital tied up in inventory. Saffron is ~₹200–600 per gram. Even 2 kg of inventory is ₹4–12 lakh of cash locked up before you sell a single jar. Import lead times (4–8 weeks) plus marketplace payment cycles (7–14 days) stretch your cash.
  4. Adulteration & authenticity risk. One bad/adulterated batch destroys a saffron brand. Test everything; build relationships with verified suppliers.
  5. Crowded, price-competitive market. Many established brands (domestic and imported). You win on brand, trust, packaging, and content — not price. Customer acquisition cost (CAC) on ads is real and rising.
  6. Marketplace dependence & fees. Amazon/Flipkart take ~15% commission + fulfillment + payment fees (~18–22% all-in). Over-relying on them caps margins; your own website is where margin lives.
  7. Seasonality & supply shocks. Saffron is harvested Oct–Nov only; prices spike on droughts and geopolitics. The 2025–26 Kashmiri crop failures pushed prices toward ₹500,000/kg — buy and hedge inventory thoughtfully.

5. How the business actually runs (operating model)

A typical monthly cycle once you're live:

  1. Source — place orders with your verified supplier/intermediary (1–4 kg at a time to start; don't over-buy).
  2. Import & clear — CHA handles customs; pay duty; receive goods.
  3. Test — pull samples, send to lab, get the crocin/ISO report.
  4. Repackage — under hygienic, FSSAI-compliant conditions: glass jars or tins, tamper-evident seal, hologram, label with batch no., weight, FSSAI licence no., MRP, your brand.
  5. List & sell — three channels:
    • Own website (Shopify/WooCommerce) — best margin, builds the asset, owns the customer.
    • Marketplaces (Amazon.in, Flipkart, BigBasket) — reach and trust, but ~18–22% all-in fees.
    • Local shops / B2B — sweet shops, gifting, HoReCa (hotels/restaurants/caterers), corporate gifting — often the best early cash flow.
  6. Market — content (saffron authenticity, recipes, health), performance ads (Meta/Google), influencers, reviews. This is where most of your variable spend goes.
  7. Fulfil & support — ship, handle returns, manage reviews. Reviews are oxygen.
  8. Reorder & analyze — track SKU-level margin, CAC, repeat rate. (Manpreet's skills make this a genuine edge — most spice founders fly blind on data.)

Product line idea: start narrow. One hero product (e.g., a beautifully packaged 2g jar) in 2–3 pack sizes (1g, 2g, 5g/10g). Add gifting boxes for Diwali/wedding season — that's where saffron sells at the best margins. Resist launching 12 SKUs on day one.

6. Unit economics (illustrative)

Assumptions: premium grade saffron, landed cost ~₹250/g (varies widely — Iranian via intermediary can be lower, Kashmiri much higher). Numbers are per 1 gram retail pack.

Line Own website Amazon.in
Selling price (realized) ₹500 ₹500
Less: marketplace + fulfilment + payment (~18–22% on Amazon; ~3–8% on website) −₹25 −₹100
Less: packaging (jar/box/label/hologram) −₹40 −₹40
Less: inbound logistics/handling (allocated) −₹15 −₹15
Less: COGS (1g landed) −₹250 −₹250
Contribution (before marketing & overhead) ₹170 (~34%) ₹95 (~19%)

Reading the table:

  • Gross/contribution margins of ~30–50% are realistic if you (a) price at a premium, (b) sell a healthy share through your own website, (c) use larger pack sizes (packaging cost per gram drops), and (d) source well.
  • Net margin after ad spend + overhead is typically 5–20% in years 1–2. Marketplace-only at low price points can hover near break-even. Saffron's high input cost compresses margins versus cheaper spices, so brand and direct-channel mix are everything.
  • The levers that move profit most: sourcing price, own-channel share, average order value / pack size, and CAC efficiency.

7. Minimum capital to start

Three honest scenarios. The biggest single variable is how much inventory you buy.

Scenario 1 — Bare-bones soft launch (~₹4–7 lakh)

  • ~1 kg inventory (₹2–3 L), minimal packaging run, DIY Shopify, tiny ad budget (₹20–40k), founders do everything, one marketplace + website. Goal: validate that people buy your brand.

Scenario 2 — Credible launch (~₹10–18 lakh) ← recommended starting point

  • 2–3 kg inventory, proper branding + packaging design, professional product photography, Amazon + Flipkart + own website, ₹1.5–3 L initial marketing, working-capital buffer, lab testing, all registrations.

Scenario 3 — Funded launch with marketing punch (~₹20–35 lakh)

  • 4–6 kg inventory, paid brand/design agency, aggressive launch marketing, gifting line, possibly a small fulfilment setup.

8. Break-even (illustrative)

  • Monthly fixed costs kept lean (founders unpaid initially, ads ₹30–80k, website + tools + misc ₹10–20k): roughly ₹50,000–1,00,000/month.
  • At ₹100–170 contribution per gram, you'd need to sell roughly 400–900 g/month to cover fixed costs — about ₹2–4.5 lakh/month in revenue.
  • Reaching that consistently typically takes 9–18 months of brand-building in a category where trust is earned slowly through reviews.
  • Payback on your initial capital: ~18–36 months in a realistic case. Treat anything faster as upside, not the plan.

Reality check: most D2C spice brands are slow, compounding businesses, not overnight wins. Profitability comes from repeat customers and an owned channel, which take 1–2 years to build. Plan your runway for at least 18 months of patience.

9. Fundraising strategy

Be clear-eyed: this is a consumer brand / trading business, not a high-growth tech startup, so the funding path is different from what you might read about in startup media.

Most realistic, in order:

  1. Bootstrap + Friends & Family. Four founders pooling ₹2.5–4.5 L each gets you to Scenario 2 without giving up equity or taking on sanctions-sensitive debt. This is genuinely the best path for the first year.
  2. MSME / Mudra loan. Once you have Udyam registration and some revenue, government-backed small-business loans (Mudra up to ₹10 L; MSME term loans) are accessible and cheap. Bhoopendra's NABARD background is a real advantage here.
  3. Government schemes. Look into PMFME (PM Formalisation of Micro Food Processing Enterprises) — credit-linked subsidy for food-processing micro-enterprises, relevant if you do domestic/Indian saffron processing. NABARD-linked agri/rural enterprise schemes may fit, especially for an Indian-saffron (Option B/C) angle. Use Bhoopendra to map these properly.
  4. Revenue-based financing / working-capital lines. Once you have consistent sales, options like inventory financing or RBF can fund inventory without diluting equity.
  5. Angel investors. Only realistic after you've shown a real brand with strong repeat rates and growth. Saffron-as-a-brand can attract D2C-focused angels, but they'll want traction first. Don't pitch on the idea alone.
  6. VC. Generally not a fit for this business unless it evolves into a broader premium-spice/wellness brand with a clear path to large scale. Don't shape the business around chasing VC.

A note on the Iran angle and funding: if you stay on Iranian sourcing (Option A), be aware that some banks and institutional investors will be uncomfortable financing an Iran-linked supply chain. This is another argument for Option B/C if external funding matters to you.

11. A sensible 90-day starting sequence

  1. Weeks 1–2: Decide the strategic fork (§1). Get the one-time sanctions/CA consult. Lock the founders' agreement and equity split.
  2. Weeks 2–4: Incorporate; apply for IEC, GST, Udyam. Shortlist brand names (see the names file) and run them through IP India Public Search.
  3. Weeks 3–6: File the trademark (Class 30). Apply for FSSAI Central Licence. Identify and vet 2–3 suppliers; get samples and lab-test them.
  4. Weeks 5–8: Finalise brand, packaging design, and one hero SKU. Set up the website.
  5. Weeks 7–10: Place first small inventory order; import; clear; test; repackage.
  6. Weeks 9–12: Soft-launch on website + Amazon.in; seed reviews; start small ad tests; approach a few local/B2B buyers for early cash flow.
  7. Then: measure SKU-level margin, CAC, and repeat rate. Double down on what works; expand pack sizes and gifting before adding new products.
